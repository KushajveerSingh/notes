## Overview

**Forms that Work Designing Web Forms for Usability** by _Caroline Jarrett_ and _Gerry Gaffney_ (2008, 1st edition).

Three layers of the form

-   **Relationship** - relationship between the organization that is asking the questions and the person who is answering.
    -   Ch-1 Persuading people to answer ("who" answers and why might do so).
    -   Ch-2 Asking for the right information ("what", "where", "when", "why": organization wants to achieve with the form).
-   **Conversation** - questions that the form asks, any other instruction, and the way the form is arranged into topics.
    -   Ch-3 Making questions easier to answer (after people are in the right mood to answer, how to ask questions the best way).
    -   Ch-4 Writing useful instructions (when you need to explain concepts on the form).
    -   Ch-5 Choosing forms controls.
    -   Ch-6 Making the form flow easily (why users make mistakes and how to help them overcome them).
-   **Appearance** - the way a form looks: the arrangement of text, input areas such as fields and graphics, and the use of color.
    -   Ch-7 Taking care of the details (where to put labels and how to indicate required fields).
    -   Ch-8 Making the form look easy (and easier to navigate).
-   Ch-9 Testing (usability testing and how to conduct the test).

## Relationship

### Ch-1 Persudaing People to Answer

A form is asking information that a user has and you do not, which represents an investment by the user. So treat this with dignity.

Users of forms can be

-   _Readers_ - that carefully read the form.
-   _Rushers_ - rush in and begin completing fields, reading only when it is necessary.
-   _Refusers_ - users that don't have anything to do with the form.

Depending upon how much value the user puts into using the website, would affect the state of the user. If you are filling tax forms with penanlties for incorrect info then you would carefully read everything, if you are signing up for a newletter you would probably just rush.
